The Burren

The Burren region is internationally famous for its landscape and flora. A visit to the Burren during the summer months will leave a person amazed by the colourful diversity of flowering plants living together within the one ecosystem. Arctic-alpine plants living side by side with Mediterranean plants, calcicole (lime-loving) and calcifuge (acid-loving) plants growing adjacent to one another and woodland plants growing out in the open with not a tree nearby to provide shade from the sun. Also found here are certain species which although rare elsewhere are abundant in the Burren. Even more amazingly they all survive in a land that appears to be composed entirely of rock.

The Burren covers 1% of the land surface of Ireland and is approximately 360 square kilometres in size. Most of the Burren is designated a Special Area of Conservation to protect this extremely unusual habitat. The Burren National Park is located in the southeastern corner of the Burren and is approximately 1500 hectares (15 square kilometres) in size. The Park land was bought by the Government for nature conservation and public access. It contains examples of all the major habitats within the Burren: Limestone Pavement, Calcareous grassland, Hazel scrub, Ash/hazel woodland, Turloughs, Lakes, Petrifying springs, Cliffs and Fen.

West of this ridge, the pavement sweeps down to partially drift-covered ground which gradually rises again to reach the foot of a rocky escarpment. To the south of the Park, the limestone bedrock disappears under a layer of glacial till. This till area is far more intensively managed for pasture and silage.

Burren Outdoor Education Centre

The Burren Outdoor Education and Training Centre is a purpose built residential centre located in the heart of the Burren and only a few minutes from the Wild Atlantic Way. They provide high quality outdoor and adventure activities to people of all ages but especially to those in the education and youth sectors.

Their programmes and activities provide rich, memorable and enjoyable outdoor experiences to a variety of learning outcomes. These include developing an active and healthy lifestyle, developing an interest in nature and the outdoors and developing an awareness and understanding of well-being and teamwork.

The unique rocky limestone landscape and location of the Burren makes it the perfect venue for a wide range of adventure activities. Over ground the karst landscape offers a selection of hillwalks from gentle strolls to more strenuous hikes over the bare rocky summits with spectacular views; the centre also offers Orienteering as a fun introduction to maps and navigation. The Burren's limestone crags are one of the most popular climbing areas in Ireland with a range of locations to choose from that will suit any ability level.

Below ground the Burren contains the most extensive network of caves to be found anywhere in Ireland, suitable for novice or experienced caver. The Burren is also bounded on two sides by sea, to the north the sheltered water and inlets of Galway Bay are ideal for novice kayakers and snorkelling; while the more exposed Atlantic coast to the west is great for body boarding and sea-kayaking.
